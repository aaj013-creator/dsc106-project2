Project 2: Persuasive/Deceptive Visualization

Dataset: Civilian Complaints Against New York City Police Officers, released by ProPublica.

Proposition: The CCRB investigation process is ineffective, as the vast majority of civilian complaints result in no disciplinary action for officers.

Positive Side: The CCRB Process Looks Ineffective

Subtitle: Total number of CCRB complaints plotted against number of complaints that ended with officer punishment.

Design decisions and rationale

  • Score: -1.0. For the “Held Accountable” bar, I filtered the dataset to complaints that ended with the officer actually being charged, labeled in the data as “Substantiated (Charges).” I excluded rows that ended in substantiation but with smaller outcomes, such as formal training or command discipline. This technically uses a real category from the data, but it is deceptive because it intentionally defines accountability in the narrowest possible way to make the successful outcome look smaller.
  • Score: -2.0. I set the y-axis minimum to 2,000 instead of 0. This visually chops off the bottom of both bars and makes the “Held Accountable” bar look even smaller relative to the total number of complaints. I considered using a logarithmic scale, but decided against it because a log scale would make the manipulation more obvious. The truncated axis creates a stronger emotional impression while still looking like a standard bar chart at first glance.
  • Score: -0.5. I used muted dark charcoal for total complaints and a vivid red for the substantiated charges bar. The gray makes the total feel neutral and institutional, while the red makes the smaller accountability bar feel urgent. This is only mildly deceptive because color emphasis is a normal design choice, but here it is being used to guide the reader toward the “accountability gap” interpretation.
  • Score: -0.5. I used a strong title, “The NYPD Accountability Gap,” and added annotations that emphasize the 89% of cases without formal charges and the 11% that ended with punitive officer punishment. The numbers are based on the data, but the wording pushes the reader toward one interpretation before they have time to consider other definitions of accountability.

Negative Side: The CCRB Process Can Be Framed as Improving

Opposing proposition: The CCRB investigation process is effective, as substantiated findings have increased substantially over time, indicating improved investigative outcomes.

Design decisions and rationale

  • Score: -0.5. I grouped the data by year_received and included every case where board_disposition contains “Substantiated.” This includes formal charges as well as smaller disciplinary actions like formal training, command discipline, and instructions. This broader definition increases the number of successful cases and makes the upward trend look stronger, so it is persuasive but still tied to a real field in the dataset.
  • Score: -1.0. I calculated the increase from 1995 to 2019 and highlighted it in the subtitle as a +718% increase. This makes the trend feel dramatic and easy to remember. However, the start year is selectively chosen because earlier years had very small values, and 2020 is not used because the dataset does not contain a full year of received complaints. This is a selective framing choice that supports the argument.
  • Score: -0.5. I used a line chart to emphasize change over time, then highlighted the years after 2010 in blue while keeping earlier years gray. The data values are not changed, but the color directs the viewer’s attention to the recent period where the increase is most visible.
  • Score: -1.0. I added an annotation near the peak that says the sharp increase suggests improved investigations. This directly tells the viewer how to interpret the trend instead of leaving the cause open-ended. It is persuasive because it connects the visual trend to the argument, but it may overstate what the chart can prove by itself.
  • Score: 0.5. I kept the axes clear and used a consistent y-axis scale starting at 0. This makes the visualization easier to read and helps it appear trustworthy, even though the framing still selectively emphasizes the more favorable story.

Final Reflection

The most straightforward part of this project was finding two true but opposite stories in the same dataset. The CCRB data makes it easy to argue that complaints rarely lead to serious accountability because only a small share of all allegations end in substantiated charges. At the same time, the data can also support a more positive argument if accountability is defined more broadly and the focus shifts to changes over time. What surprised me was how much the conclusion changed depending on whether I used counts, percentages, narrow categories, broad categories, or a time trend.

The hardest part was deciding where persuasion becomes misleading. For example, filtering to only “Substantiated (Charges)” is not fabricated, but it hides other forms of substantiated discipline. Similarly, highlighting the rise in substantiated findings over time is accurate, but it does not prove that the entire investigation process is effective. These choices showed me that ethical visualization is not just about avoiding fake numbers. It also means being honest about definitions, missing context, and what the chart can or cannot prove.

After making both sides, I would define ethical analysis and visualization as presenting data in a way that is clear about its transformations and does not rely on hiding important context from the reader. Persuasive choices can be acceptable when they help viewers understand a real pattern, such as using annotations, color, or grouping. They become misleading when the design depends on the viewer not noticing a key decision, such as a truncated axis or an intentionally narrow definition of accountability. Overall, I lean toward the first side of the proposition because the overall share of complaints leading to formal charges is low, but the second visualization shows that the same dataset can still be framed as evidence of improvement.
